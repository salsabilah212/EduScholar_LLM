TITLE: Constructivism Theory - Foundation of Modern Learning
AUTHORS: Jean Piaget, Lev Vygotsky, Ernst von Glasersfeld
YEAR: 1970-2020

1.1 INTRODUCTION

Constructivism is one of the most influential learning theories in 21st-century education. This theory argues that knowledge cannot be simply transferred from teacher to student, but must be actively constructed by students through experience, interaction, and reflection. The development of this theory began with Jean Piaget's work on children's cognitive development and Lev Vygotsky's work on social interaction in learning.

The constructivist paradigm emerged as a critique of behaviorism, which viewed learning as behavioral change resulting from stimulus-response. Constructivism emphasizes that each individual has a unique way of interpreting the world based on existing knowledge schemas.

1.2 COGNITIVE CONSTRUCTIVISM (PIAGET)

Piaget identified four stages of cognitive development that influence how students learn:
- Sensorimotor (0-2 years): learning through senses and movement
- Preoperational (2-7 years): beginning to use symbols and language
- Concrete Operational (7-11 years): able to think logically about physical objects
- Formal Operational (11+ years): able to think abstractly and hypothetically

Key concepts from Piaget:
1. Schema: mental structures for organizing knowledge
2. Assimilation: incorporating new information into existing schemas
3. Accommodation: modifying schemas when new information doesn't fit
4. Equilibration: the balancing process between assimilation and accommodation

Learning occurs when there is disequilibrium that pushes students to accommodate new information. The teacher's role is to create challenging yet achievable situations.

1.3 SOCIAL CONSTRUCTIVISM (VYGOTSKY)

Vygotsky emphasized that learning is a social process occurring through interaction with others. This theory differs from Piaget's which focused more on individual internal processes.

Three main concepts from Vygotsky:
1. Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD): The distance between actual ability (done independently) and potential ability (with assistance)
2. Scaffolding: Support provided by adults or more capable peers, gradually reduced as student competence increases
3. Private Speech: Internal speech that helps children regulate their own thinking

Practical implications: Collaborative learning, peer tutoring, and teachers as facilitators providing appropriate scaffolding within the ZPD.

1.4 RADICAL CONSTRUCTIVISM (VON GLASERSFELD)

Von Glasersfeld introduced radical constructivism which emphasizes that knowledge is not a representation of objective reality, but a personal construction useful for solving problems. Knowledge is "viable" if it functions in a particular context, not "true" in an absolute sense.

1.5 IMPLICATIONS FOR TEACHING PRACTICE

Constructivism-based learning strategies:
1. Problem-Based Learning (PBL): students solve authentic problems
2. Project-Based Learning: students produce real products
3. Inquiry-Based Learning: students discover through investigation
4. Collaborative Learning: discussion and group work

Teacher's role:
- Provide a resource-rich learning environment
- Ask open-ended questions that stimulate thinking
- Provide guiding feedback, not correcting feedback
- Assess process and product, not just final results

1.6 CRITICISM AND CHALLENGES

Criticism of constructivism:
- Too idealistic and difficult to implement in large classes
- Requires more time than direct instruction
- Not all material is suitable for constructivist approaches
- Difficult to objectively measure learning achievement

However, meta-analysis research shows that constructivist approaches improve conceptual understanding (effect size 0.65) and critical thinking skills (effect size 0.58) compared to direct instruction (Hattie, 2009).

TITLE: Cognitivism Theory - Understanding Thinking Processes in Learning
AUTHORS: Jerome Bruner, Robert Gagné, Benjamin Bloom
YEAR: 1956-2000

2.1 INTRODUCTION

Cognitivism is a learning theory that focuses on internal mental processes such as perception, memory, thinking, and problem-solving. Unlike behaviorism which only observes visible behavior, cognitivism attempts to understand what happens "inside the head" of students during learning.

This theory developed in the 1950s and 1960s as a reaction to behaviorism. Key figures include Jerome Bruner, Robert Gagné, and Benjamin Bloom. Cognitivism views the human brain like a computer processing information - input, process, output.

2.2 INFORMATION PROCESSING MODEL

The Atkinson-Shiffrin model (1968) explains three types of memory:
1. Sensory Memory: holds information very briefly (less than 1 second)
2. Short-Term Memory / Working Memory: limited capacity (7±2 items), duration ~20 seconds without rehearsal
3. Long-Term Memory: unlimited capacity, permanent duration

Learning occurs when information moves from sensory to short-term, then to long-term memory through encoding. Retrieval is the process of taking information back from long-term memory to working memory.

2.3 BLOOM'S TAXONOMY

Benjamin Bloom developed a taxonomy of educational objectives classifying levels of thinking:
1. Remember: recalling facts and basic concepts
2. Understand: explaining ideas or concepts
3. Apply: using information in new situations
4. Analyze: distinguishing parts and their relationships
5. Evaluate: making judgments based on criteria
6. Create: producing new products or ideas

Bloom's Taxonomy was revised (Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001) into a two-dimensional version: knowledge dimension (factual, conceptual, procedural, metacognitive) and cognitive process dimension (remember, understand, apply, analyze, evaluate, create).

2.4 COGNITIVE LOAD THEORY

John Sweller (1988) developed cognitive load theory which explains that working memory has limited capacity. There are three types of cognitive load:
1. Intrinsic Load: complexity of the material being learned (cannot be changed)
2. Extraneous Load: how the material is delivered (can be optimized)
3. Germane Load: mental effort to build schemas (desirable)

Instructional design principles based on CLT:
- Avoid irrelevant information (reduce extraneous load)
- Use diagrams and visualizations (modality effect)
- Break complex material into small parts (segmentation)
- Use worked examples for beginners

2.5 IMPLICATIONS FOR TEACHING

Cognitivism-based strategies:
1. Advance Organizer: providing a framework before details
2. Mnemonic Devices: memory techniques (acronyms, visual associations)
3. Elaboration: connecting new information with prior knowledge
4. Organization: arranging information in logical structures
5. Practice and Feedback: practice with immediate feedback

Teachers need to consider students' working memory capacity and design learning that minimizes unnecessary cognitive load.

TITLE: Behaviorism Theory and Its Applications in Education
AUTHORS: Ivan Pavlov, B.F. Skinner, John B. Watson
YEAR: 1900-1970

3.1 INTRODUCTION

Behaviorism is a learning theory that focuses on observable and measurable behavior. This theory ignores internal mental processes and only considers the relationship between stimulus and response. Behaviorism was highly influential in the 20th century and is still used in various aspects of education such as classroom management and programmed instruction.

3.2 CLASSICAL CONDITIONING (PAVLOV)

Ivan Pavlov (1849-1936) discovered classical conditioning through experiments with dogs. Key concepts:
- Unconditioned Stimulus (US): natural stimulus that triggers a response (food)
- Unconditioned Response (UR): natural response (salivation)
- Conditioned Stimulus (CS): neutral stimulus that becomes associated (bell)
- Conditioned Response (CR): learned response (salivation when hearing the bell)

Applications in education:
- Creating positive associations with the learning environment (comfortable classroom)
- Overcoming math anxiety through systematic desensitization
- Building consistent classroom routines

3.3 OPERANT CONDITIONING (SKINNER)

B.F. Skinner (1904-1990) developed operant conditioning which focuses on behavioral consequences:
- Reinforcement: increases the likelihood of behavior being repeated
  - Positive reinforcement: adding something pleasant (praise, good grades)
  - Negative reinforcement: removing something unpleasant (removing extra assignments)
- Punishment: decreases the likelihood of behavior being repeated
  - Positive punishment: adding something unpleasant (reprimand)
  - Negative punishment: removing something pleasant (taking away privileges)

Skinner developed the concepts of shaping (gradual behavior formation) and programmed instruction (programmed learning with immediate feedback).

3.4 BANDURA'S SOCIAL LEARNING THEORY

Albert Bandura (1925-2021) bridged behaviorism and cognitivism with social learning theory. Key concepts:
- Observational Learning: learning through observing models
- Vicarious Reinforcement: learning from consequences experienced by others
- Self-Efficacy: one's belief in their own abilities

The Bobo Doll experiment showed that children imitate observed aggressive behavior. Implication: the importance of positive modeling by teachers and parents.

3.5 EDUCATIONAL IMPLICATIONS

Behaviorism applications in the classroom:
1. Token Economy: token-based reward system (points, stickers)
2. Behavior Contract: contract between teacher and student about expected behavior
3. Direct Instruction: structured teaching with immediate feedback
4. Mastery Learning: students must achieve criteria before moving on
5. Classroom Management: using reinforcement for positive behavior

Criticism of behaviorism: too mechanistic, ignores social and emotional aspects, lacks attention to critical thinking and creativity.

TITLE: Quantitative Educational Research - Design, Methods, and Analysis
AUTHORS: John W. Creswell, J.C. Nunnally
YEAR: 2014-2022

4.1 DEFINITION AND CHARACTERISTICS

Quantitative research in education is a systematic approach to testing theories by measuring variables numerically and analyzing data using statistical procedures. Main characteristics:
- Focuses on objective measurement
- Uses representative samples
- Prioritizes generalization of findings
- Uses standardized instruments (questionnaires, tests)
- Data analysis based on inferential statistics

Objectives of quantitative research: testing hypotheses, predicting relationships, explaining cause-and-effect, and generalizing findings to populations.

4.2 QUANTITATIVE RESEARCH DESIGNS

A. Experimental Designs
1. True Experiment: random assignment to control and experimental groups. Has high internal validity.
   - Pre-test Post-test Control Group Design
   - Post-test Only Control Group Design
2. Quasi-Experiment: without random assignment. More practical for educational settings.
   - Non-equivalent Control Group Design
   - Time Series Design

B. Non-Experimental Designs
1. Correlational Design: measuring relationships between two or more variables
   - Bivariate correlation (Pearson r)
   - Partial correlation (controlling for third variable)
   - Multiple regression (prediction with multiple predictors)

2. Survey Design: collecting data from samples through structured questionnaires/interviews
   - Cross-sectional (single time)
   - Longitudinal (periodic)

3. Ex Post Facto: examining cause-effect that has already occurred without researcher intervention

4.3 SAMPLING TECHNIQUES

Probability Sampling:
- Random Sampling: every member of population has equal chance
- Stratified Sampling: dividing population into strata, sampling from each stratum
- Cluster Sampling: sampling groups (classes, schools) rather than individuals

Non-Probability Sampling:
- Convenience Sampling: based on availability (high bias risk)
- Purposive Sampling: based on specific criteria
- Snowball Sampling: through participant recommendations

4.4 VALIDITY AND RELIABILITY

Validity: whether the instrument measures what it is supposed to measure
1. Content Validity: extent to which instrument content represents the construct
2. Criterion Validity: correlation with external criteria
3. Construct Validity: extent to which it measures the theoretical construct

Reliability: consistency of measurement results
1. Test-Retest: consistency over time
2. Internal Consistency: consistency among items (Cronbach's alpha)
3. Inter-rater: consistency among assessors

4.5 DATA ANALYSIS

Descriptive Statistics:
- Mean, median, mode
- Standard deviation, variance, range
- Frequencies, percentages, graphs

Inferential Statistics:
- T-test (independent/paired): comparing 2 groups
- ANOVA: comparing 3+ groups
- ANCOVA: ANOVA with covariate
- MANOVA: multiple dependent variables
- Linear/Logistic Regression: prediction
- Pearson/Spearman Correlation: relationships between variables

Assumption Tests: normality (Kolmogorov-Smirnov), homogeneity (Levene), linearity

4.6 ADVANTAGES AND DISADVANTAGES

Advantages: objective, strong generalization, easy replication, advanced statistical analysis
Disadvantages: ignores context, data limited to instruments, does not capture deeper meaning

TITLE: Qualitative Research - Understanding Meaning in Educational Context
AUTHORS: Sharan Merriam, Robert Stake, Michael Patton
YEAR: 2009-2022

5.1 DEFINITION AND CHARACTERISTICS

Qualitative research is an approach to understanding social phenomena from the participants' perspective in natural settings. The researcher becomes the primary instrument, collecting data through direct interaction with participants and interpreting the meaning behind behavior and experience.

Characteristics of qualitative research:
- Naturalistic inquiry: research in natural settings
- Interpretive: researcher interprets data
- Holistic: viewing phenomena comprehensively
- Emic: understanding from participant's perspective
- Flexible: design evolves during research

5.2 QUALITATIVE APPROACHES/PARADIGMS

A. Phenomenology: Focuses on the lived experience of individuals. Aims to understand the essence of common experience.

B. Ethnography: Focuses on culture and practices within communities. Researcher engages directly in the setting.

C. Case Study: Intensive investigation of a single case (individual, group, program) in a real-world context.

D. Grounded Theory: Developing theory from data (bottom-up), not testing existing theories.

E. Narrative: Examining personal stories and experiences in narrative form.

5.3 DATA COLLECTION TECHNIQUES

1. In-depth Interviews: unstructured, semi-structured, or structured
2. Participant Observation: observing and participating in the setting
3. Document Analysis: analyzing personal and official documents
4. Focus Group Discussion: 6-10 participants with similar experiences

5.4 TRUSTWORTHINESS

Credibility (internal validity): Triangulation, member checking, peer debriefing, prolonged engagement
Transferability (external validity): Thick description, appropriate purposive sampling
Dependability (reliability): Audit trail, reflexivity journal
Confirmability (objectivity): Confirmability audit, reflexivity

5.5 QUALITATIVE DATA ANALYSIS

Analysis steps:
1. Data preparation: transcription, data organization
2. Reading and memoing: reading and writing reflection notes
3. Coding: labeling data segments (open, axial, selective coding)
4. Theming: identifying patterns and themes
5. Interpretation: explaining meaning

Supporting software: NVivo, ATLAS.ti, MaxQDA

5.6 ADVANTAGES AND DISADVANTAGES

Advantages: rich and deep data, flexible, preserves context
Disadvantages: limited generalization, subjective, time and cost intensive

TITLE: Classroom Action Research - Teacher as Researcher
AUTHORS: Stephen Kemmis, Robin McTaggart, John Elliot
YEAR: 1988-2020

6.1 DEFINITION AND CHARACTERISTICS

Classroom Action Research (CAR) is research conducted by teachers in their own classrooms to improve the quality of learning. CAR is participatory, collaborative, and reflective.

Characteristics of CAR:
- Practical: directly applied in the classroom
- Situational: contextual to real conditions
- Reflective: continuous evaluation and improvement
- Participatory: teacher as primary researcher
- Cyclical: conducted in repeating cycles

6.2 CAR MODELS

A. Kemmis & McTaggart Model (1988): Spiral model with 4 steps: Plan, Act, Observe, Reflect

B. John Elliot Model (1991): Modification with more detailed steps

C. Kurt Lewin Model: Planning -> Action -> Fact-finding

D. McNiff & Whitehead Model (2000): Emphasizes "living theory"

6.3 CAR STEPS

1. Problem Identification: initial observation, identifying learning problems
2. Analysis and Diagnosis: analyzing causes, literature review
3. Action Planning: determining strategies, preparing instruments, setting indicators
4. Action Implementation: applying strategies, collecting data
5. Observation and Monitoring: observing process, recording findings
6. Reflection: analyzing data, assessing success, identifying improvements

6.4 INSTRUMENTS AND DATA COLLECTION

Quantitative Data: Achievement tests, questionnaires, attitude scales
Qualitative Data: Observation sheets, interviews, document analysis, reflective journal

6.5 SUCCESS CRITERIA

Success indicators must be SMART: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound

6.6 ETHICS IN CAR

Informed consent, identity confidentiality, not harming students, reporting honestly

6.7 CAR EXAMPLE

Title: Improving Students' Critical Thinking Skills Through Problem-Based Learning in Science

Cycle I: Plan -> Act -> Observe (score 65) -> Reflect (need scaffolding)
Cycle II: Plan -> Act -> Observe (score 78) -> Reflect (improved, group work needs improvement)
Cycle III: Plan -> Act -> Observe (score 85) -> Reflect (target achieved)

TITLE: Character Education - Theory, Practice, and Policy
AUTHORS: Thomas Lickona, Marvin Berkowitz, Indonesian Ministry of Education
YEAR: 1991-2022

7.1 INTRODUCTION

Character education is a conscious and systematic effort to help students understand, care about, and act according to basic moral values. Character education is becoming increasingly important amid moral decadence, bullying, and identity crises among youth.

In Indonesia, character education became a national priority through the Character Strengthening Education Movement (PPK) which began in 2016.

7.2 COMPONENTS OF CHARACTER EDUCATION (THOMAS LICKONA)

Thomas Lickona (1991) identified three interrelated moral components:

1. Moral Knowing: moral awareness, knowing moral values, perspective-taking, moral reasoning, decision-making

2. Moral Feeling: conscience, self-esteem, empathy, loving the good, self-control

3. Moral Action: competence, will, habit

Comprehensive character education must develop all three components in balance.

7.3 CHARACTER PILLARS (INDONESIAN MINISTRY OF EDUCATION)

The Ministry of Education and Culture identifies 5 main character values:
1. Religious: accepting teachings, practicing worship, tolerance
2. Nationalist: love for homeland, appreciating diversity, prioritizing unity
3. Independent: not dependent on others, responsible, able to solve problems
4. Mutual Cooperation: collaboration, caring, sharing
5. Integrity: honesty, keeping promises, responsible for actions

7.4 IMPLEMENTATION STRATEGIES IN SCHOOLS

1. School Culture: positive atmosphere, role modeling, positive traditions
2. Curriculum Integration: in subjects, special subjects, projects
3. Regular Habits: morning handshakes, prayers, clean Friday
4. Extracurricular Activities: scouts, Red Cross, social activities
5. Collaboration with Parents: aligned values, regular communication

7.5 RESEARCH ON CHARACTER EDUCATION

Meta-analysis of 100+ studies (Berkowitz & Bier, 2005) shows effective programs have:
- Minimum duration of 1 year
- Involves entire school community
- Combines values teaching with practice
- Opportunities for moral discussion
- Consistency across aspects

Average effect size: d = 0.30 for prosocial behavior.

7.6 CHALLENGES IN CHARACTER EDUCATION

1. Inconsistency between taught values and teacher behavior
2. Influence of external environments (media, peers)
3. Lack of time and priority
4. Measuring abstract and subjective character
5. Value differences across cultures

--- DOCUMENT 8 ---
TITLE: Child Moral and Social Development - Kohlberg's Theory and Its Influence
AUTHORS: Lawrence Kohlberg, Carol Gilligan
YEAR: 1958-1990

8.1 KOHLBERG'S THEORY OF MORAL DEVELOPMENT

Lawrence Kohlberg (1927-1987) developed a theory of moral development consisting of 3 levels and 6 stages.

Level I: Pre-conventional Morality (ages 4-10): Focuses on physical consequences and self-interest
- Stage 1: Punishment and Obedience Orientation
- Stage 2: Instrumental Hedonistic Orientation

Level II: Conventional Morality (ages 10-15): Focuses on adherence to social rules
- Stage 3: "Good Boy/Good Girl" / Interpersonal Orientation
- Stage 4: Law and Order Orientation

Level III: Post-conventional / Principled Morality (ages 16+): Focuses on universal moral principles
- Stage 5: Social Contract Orientation
- Stage 6: Universal Ethical Principles Orientation

8.2 CRITICISM OF KOHLBERG'S THEORY

Carol Gilligan (1982) criticized Kohlberg because:
- Theory was based only on male subjects
- Ignores "care ethics" perspective
- Women tend to be at lower stages due to different approaches

Other criticisms: Stage 6 is rarely achieved, too focused on reasoning, lacks cultural considerations.

8.3 IMPLICATIONS FOR EDUCATION

1. Moral Dilemma Discussions: stimulate moral reasoning development
2. Just Community: schools as democratic communities
3. Role Play: acting out roles to understand others' perspectives
4. Reflection: encouraging students to reflect on their values

8.4 SOCIAL DEVELOPMENT

- Erikson: 8 stages of psychosocial development
- Piaget: development of social understanding
- Vygotsky: social interaction as the engine of development

TITLE: Educational Technology - Evolution, Theory, and Practice
AUTHORS: Robert Gagné, Richard Mayer, AECT
YEAR: 1965-2023

9.1 DEFINITION AND EVOLUTION

Educational technology is defined by AECT as "the study and ethical practice of facilitating learning and improving performance by creating, using, and managing appropriate technological processes and resources."

Evolution of educational technology:
- 1960-70: Mainframe computers, programmed instruction
- 1980-90: Personal computers, educational software
- 2000-2010: Web 2.0, e-learning, LMS
- 2010-2020: Mobile learning, MOOCs, AI
- 2020+: Generative AI, VR/AR, adaptive learning

9.2 FOUNDATIONAL THEORIES

A. Cognitive Load Theory (Sweller): Reduce extraneous load, manage intrinsic load

B. Multimedia Learning (Mayer): 12 principles including multimedia, contiguity, modality, redundancy, coherence, signaling, segmenting, pre-training, personalization, voice, image, embodiment

C. Gagné's Nine Events of Instruction:
1. Gain attention
2. Inform objectives
3. Stimulate recall
4. Present content
5. Provide guidance
6. Elicit performance
7. Provide feedback
8. Assess performance
9. Enhance retention and transfer

9.3 TYPES OF LEARNING MEDIA

Visual Media: Infographics, diagrams, posters, videos
Audio Media: Podcasts, recordings, music
Interactive Digital Media: Simulations, games, AR, VR
Learning Platforms: LMS (Moodle, Canvas), MOOCs (Coursera, edX), Social Learning (Edmodo)

9.4 BLENDED LEARNING AND HYBRID LEARNING

Blended Learning: combination of online and face-to-face. Effect size 0.35.

Models: Station Rotation, Flipped Classroom, Flex Model, Enriched Virtual

9.5 CHALLENGES IN EDUCATIONAL TECHNOLOGY

1. Access gap (digital divide)
2. Teacher digital literacy
3. Data security and privacy
4. Technology distraction
5. Implementation costs
6. Difficult to evaluate effectiveness

9.6 FUTURE TRENDS

- Adaptive learning: AI adjusting material to student ability
- Generative AI: ChatGPT, AI tutors
- Microlearning: small modules for flexible learning
- Immersive learning: VR/AR
- Learning Analytics: data analysis for prediction

TITLE: Learning Motivation - Theories and Strategies for Improvement
AUTHORS: Abraham Maslow, Edward Deci, Richard Ryan, Carole Dweck
YEAR: 1943-2020

11.1 INTRODUCTION

Motivation is the driving force that directs, activates, and sustains learning behavior. Learning motivation is one of the strongest predictors of academic achievement, even stronger than IQ in some studies.

Motivation is divided into:
- Intrinsic Motivation: internal drive (curiosity, interest)
- Extrinsic Motivation: external drive (grades, rewards, praise)

Both are important, but intrinsic motivation is more sustainable and correlates more strongly with long-term achievement.

11.2 MASLOW'S HIERARCHY OF NEEDS

Abraham Maslow (1943) proposed a hierarchy of 5 needs:
1. Physiological: food, water, rest (basic)
2. Safety: physical safety, stability, health
3. Social: affection, friendship, belonging
4. Esteem: recognition, respect, status
5. Self-Actualization: reaching full potential

Principle: basic needs must be met before higher-level needs can be activated.

11.3 SELF-DETERMINATION THEORY (DECI & RYAN)

SDT identifies three basic psychological needs for intrinsic motivation:
1. Autonomy: feeling of having control and choice
2. Competence: feeling capable and effective
3. Relatedness: feeling connected and accepted by others

When these three needs are met, intrinsic motivation naturally increases.

11.4 EXPECTANCY-VALUE THEORY (ECCLES)

Motivation depends on two factors:
1. Expectancy of Success: confidence in success
2. Task Value: how valuable the task is

Task value consists of: attainment value, intrinsic value, utility value, cost

11.5 THEORY MINDSET (CAROL DWECK)

Fixed Mindset: believes ability is innate and unchangeable
Growth Mindset: believes ability can develop through effort

Growth mindset interventions can improve motivation and achievement.

11.6 THEORY OF ATTRIBUTION (WEINER)

Students attribute success/failure to:
- Internal vs External
- Stable vs Unstable
- Controllable vs Uncontrollable

Adaptive attribution: attributing failure to lack of effort (internal, unstable, controllable)

11.7 STRATEGIES TO INCREASE STUDENT MOTIVATION

1. Create a safe and supportive learning environment
2. Provide choice and autonomy in tasks
3. Set specific, challenging, and achievable goals
4. Provide constructive and specific feedback
5. Build relevance: connect material to student life
6. Use varied teaching methods
7. Reward effort and progress
8. Develop growth mindset with appropriate language
9. Facilitate collaboration and social interaction
10. Recognize and appreciate individual interests

11.8 MOTIVATION RESEARCH

Meta-analysis by Hattie (2009): correlation between motivation and achievement r = 0.34. Students with high intrinsic motivation are 2.5x more likely to persist in difficult tasks.

3. Positive Learning Environment: good teacher-student relationships, supportive class norms

5. Increasing Self-Efficacy: success experiences, modeling and scaffolding

12.3 LEARNING READINESS

Influenced by: Cognitive (prior knowledge), Affective (motivation and interest), Psychomotor (basic skills)

Principle: learning should start from the student's ZPD.

12.4 THE TEACHER'S ROLE IN MOTIVATION

1. Positive expectations: believes all students can learn
2. Enthusiasm: passion in teaching is contagious
3. Fairness: fair and consistent treatment
4. Humor: relaxed and enjoyable atmosphere
5. Personalization: knowing students individually

12.5 PRACTICAL APPLICATIONS IN THE CLASSROOM

1. "What's in it for me?" - explain relevance at the beginning
2. Choice boards - task/project choices
3. Peer tutoring - learning from peers
4. Gamification - points, badges, leaderboards
5. Real-world connections - industry visits, guest speakers

TITLE: Learning Assessment - Principles and Practice
AUTHORS: Paul Black, Dylan Wiliam, Grant Wiggins
YEAR: 1998-2022

13.1 THE ROLE OF ASSESSMENT IN LEARNING

Assessment is a systematic process of collecting and interpreting information about student learning. Good assessment not only measures results but also guides the learning process.

13.2 FORMATIVE ASSESSMENT

Formative assessment is "assessment for learning" - used during the learning process to provide feedback and improve learning.

Characteristics: Informal and continuous, not for final grade, focus on identifying gaps, actionable feedback, involves students (self/peer assessment)

Examples: Short quizzes, oral questions, exit tickets, thumbs up/down, student portfolios, reflective journals

13.3 SUMMATIVE ASSESSMENT

Summative assessment is "assessment of learning" - used at the end of a period to evaluate learning achievement and give grades.

Characteristics: Formal and structured, produces grades, at the end of unit/semester/year, measures final achievement

Examples: Final exams, national exams, final projects, practical exams

13.4 RUBRICS

Rubrics are assessment guidelines that explain criteria and performance levels.

Types: Holistic (overall score) vs Analytic (per criteria/dimension)

Components: Criteria, Descriptors, Scale

13.5 EFFECTIVE FEEDBACK

Characteristics:
1. Specific: shows what is good and what needs improvement
2. Timely: given immediately after performance
3. Focus on task, not person
4. Follow-up: provides concrete suggestions for improvement
5. Comparison with criteria, not with other students

Feedback model: Feed-up (goals), Feed-back (current position), Feed-forward (next steps)

13.6 PRINCIPLES OF GOOD ASSESSMENT

1. Clear purpose
2. Clear targets
3. Sound design (valid and reliable)
4. Effective communication
5. Student involvement

13.7 AUTHENTIC ASSESSMENT

Assesses student ability in tasks that resemble the real world.

Characteristics: Complex and challenging, requires application of knowledge, involves process and product, allows multiple solutions

Examples: research projects, presentations, portfolios, simulations, practical work

TITLE: Basic Statistics and Data Analysis for Educational Research
AUTHORS: Andy Field, J.C. Nunnally
YEAR: 2013-2022

15.1 DESCRIPTIVE STATISTICS

Used to describe and summarize data.

Measures of Central Tendency:
- Mean (μ): average = Σx/n
- Median: middle value after sorting
- Mode: most frequent value

Measures of Dispersion:
- Range: max - min
- Variance (σ²): average squared deviation from mean
- Standard deviation (σ): square root of variance
- Interquartile range (IQR): Q3 - Q1

Data Visualization: Histogram, Box plot, Scatter plot, Bar chart

15.2 INFERENTIAL STATISTICS

Used to generalize from sample to population and test hypotheses.

Basic Concepts: Population vs Sample, Parameter vs Statistic, Null (H₀) and Alternative (H₁) hypotheses, Significance level (α = 0.05), p-value

Parametric Tests:
1. T-test: One-sample, Independent, Paired
2. ANOVA: One-way, Two-way, Repeated-measures
3. Pearson Correlation (r): 0.1-0.3 weak, 0.3-0.5 moderate, 0.5+ strong
4. Linear Regression: Simple (Y = a + bX), Multiple (Y = a + b₁X₁ + b₂X₂ + ...)

Non-parametric Tests: Mann-Whitney U, Wilcoxon, Kruskal-Wallis, Chi-Square

15.3 EFFECT SIZE

Measures the magnitude of impact, independent of sample size.

Types:
1. Cohen's d: d = 0.2 (small), 0.5 (medium), 0.8 (large)
2. Eta-squared (η²): 0.01 (small), 0.06 (medium), 0.14 (large)
3. Correlation r: 0.1 (small), 0.3 (medium), 0.5 (large)

Interpretation: effect size is more important than p-value for practical significance.

15.4 ASSUMPTION TESTS

For parametric tests: Normality (Kolmogorov-Smirnov, Shapiro-Wilk), Homogeneity of variance (Levene's test), Linearity, Independence

15.5 ADVANCED ANALYSIS TECHNIQUES

1. ANCOVA: ANOVA + covariate
2. MANOVA: multiple dependent variables
3. Logistic Regression: categorical outcome
4. Factor Analysis: dimension reduction
5. Structural Equation Modeling (SEM): complex models

15.6 STATISTICAL SOFTWARE

Popular software: SPSS, R, JASP, JAMOVI, Python (pandas, scipy, statsmodels)

15.7 INTERPRETING STATISTICAL RESULTS

1. Are assumptions met?
2. What is the test statistic (t, F, χ²) and p-value?
3. Is it significant? (p < α)
4. What is the effect size?
5. Interpret in research context

TITLE: Comparison of Quantitative and Qualitative Methodology
AUTHORS: Creswell, Denzin & Lincoln
YEAR: 2010-2022

17.1 EPISTEMOLOGICAL COMPARISON

Quantitative Research:
- Positivist/realist: objective reality, measurable
- Researcher separate from subjects
- Generalization and prediction
- Deductive (theory -> data)

Qualitative Research:
- Interpretive/constructivist: reality socially constructed
- Researcher involved and interactive
- Deep understanding
- Inductive (data -> theory)

17.2 METHODOLOGICAL COMPARISON

| Aspect | Quantitative | Qualitative |
|--------|--------------|-------------|
| Questions | How many? Relationships? | How? Why? |
| Sample | Large, representative | Small, purposive |
| Instruments | Tests, questionnaires | Interviews, observation |
| Data | Numeric | Text, images, audio |
| Analysis | Statistical | Thematic, narrative |
| Purpose | Generalization | Contextual understanding |

17.3 MIXED METHODS RESEARCH

Combines quantitative and qualitative approaches.

Designs:
1. Convergent: both quantitative and qualitative, collected simultaneously, compared
2. Explanatory sequential: quantitative first, qualitative explains results
3. Exploratory sequential: qualitative first, quantitative tests findings

17.4 RESEARCH QUALITY CRITERIA

Quantitative: Internal validity, External validity, Reliability
Qualitative: Credibility, Transferability, Dependability, Confirmability

17.5 WHEN TO USE?

Choose Quantitative if:
- Want to test hypotheses
- Variables can be measured
- Large sample available
- Want generalization

Choose Qualitative if:
- Want to understand experience/meaning
- Phenomenon is not well understood
- Context is important
- Want rich data

Choose Mixed Methods if:
- Single approach is not enough
- Want triangulation
- Phenomenon complexity requires both approaches
